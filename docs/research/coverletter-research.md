# Cover Letter Best Practices for Senior Software Engineers in International Tech Hiring

## Key principles

- Treat the cover letter as a writing sample used during screening, and state role fit in a direct way.
- Tailor each letter to a specific role and organization by using job posting requirements and role context as the content driver.
- Put the exact position name in the first paragraph, and keep the opening short.
- Use concrete achievements to support claims, and add numbers or measurable outcomes when available.
- Focus on achievements and results, not a list of day-to-day duties.
- Keep writing concise, factual, and within a single page.
- Address the letter to a specific person when possible, else use a professional fallback greeting.
- Avoid repeating the resume in paragraph form, expand on one or two relevant projects or accomplishments and connect each to job requirements.
- Include a closing paragraph with a clear call to action about next steps, plus a professional sign-off.
- Keep formatting aligned with the resume, including matching header and font choices.

## Recommended structure

- **Header block:** Name, email, phone, location, and optional portfolio links (GitHub, personal site) in a business-letter header style.
- **Greeting:** “Dear {Hiring Manager Name}” when a name exists, or “Dear Hiring Manager” as fallback.
- **Opening paragraph (one to two sentences):** State “applying for {Role}”, reference where the posting appeared, and show specific interest in the role and the organization.
- **Proof of impact and relevance (body paragraph one):** Select one or two achievements with measurable outcomes, and map each to top requirements in the posting, including relevant keywords.
- **Company fit (body paragraph two):** Show knowledge of mission, values, or business context, and explain alignment between goals and role needs.
- **Closing paragraph and call to action:** Restate interest and fit, thank the reader, and request next steps such as an interview or discussion.
- **Signature:** Professional closing phrase plus name (avoid adding a second email signature block).

## Ideal length and formatting

- **Length range:** Half a page to one full page.
- **Maximum length:** Do not exceed one page.
- **Word-count guideline when a posting gives no limit:** 250 to 400 words.
- **Paragraph count:** Three to four paragraphs, and Indeed also states “limit…to 4 paragraphs.”
- **Reading-time framing from a major hiring platform:** Divide content into three or four short paragraphs “read in around 10 seconds or less.”
- **Recruiter-stated length preferences from a recruiter poll:** 49% prefer half a page, 26% prefer one page, 25% prefer “a few sentences long.”
- **Formatting details (business-letter formatting):** One page, simple professional font (examples given include Arial or Helvetica), 10 to 12 point size, left alignment, single spacing, one-inch margins.
- **Header and style alignment:** Reuse the same header style across resume and cover letter, and keep formatting consistent.
- **Online application form exception:** When pasting a cover letter into an online form that already collects contact details, Indeed states no need to include address or other contact details again in the letter header.

## What recruiters care about

- **Role-requirement match:** A recruiter poll reports the top cover-letter focus as an applicant’s ability to connect work experience with role demands.
- **Communication quality:** The same recruiter poll reports recruiter focus on personality plus ability to communicate, and Harvard describes the cover letter as a writing sample used in screening.
- **Evidence-based claims:** Sources recommend backing claims with concrete accomplishments, including quantification and data where available.
- **Motivation and fit:** Multiple sources frame the cover letter as a place to show interest in the role and explain fit between candidate and organization.
- **Job-description comprehension:** A recruiter poll lists “carefully read the job description” and “motivation for applying” among recruiter focus areas.
- **Referrals or connections:** A recruiter poll lists referral or other connection to the company as a cover-letter scan focus area for a meaningful share of recruiters.
- **Keyword matching and ATS behavior:** Harvard and Indeed both describe applicant tracking systems scanning cover letters for keywords or phrases, and both recommend including relevant keywords from the job description.
- **High-stakes downside risk:** A recruiter poll reports 81% of recruiters rejected candidates “based solely on their cover letters,” which raises the cost of errors and generic content.
- **Cover-letter review variance:** Berkeley states cover-letter reading varies by recruiter and hiring manager, with some focusing on the resume.
- **High-volume reading behavior:** MIT EECS Communication Lab describes readers reviewing many letters and seeking a fast yes or no decision, which rewards clear structure and fast proof.

## Common mistakes

- Submitting a generic letter across roles instead of tailoring content to the specific position and employer.
- Repeating resume content instead of expanding with detail on selected achievements or projects.
- Focusing on duties rather than outcomes and achievements.
- Making claims without support from facts, metrics, or examples.
- Discussing salary expectations without an explicit employer request.
- Writing about reasons for leaving or other negative details, rather than keeping content future-focused and role-focused.
- Using “To Whom It May Concern,” rather than a targeted greeting or “Dear Hiring Manager.”
- Skipping proofreading, since multiple sources treat spelling or grammar errors as disqualifying signals.
- Omitting a call to action in the closing, since Indeed lists this as a mistake and also defines a call to action as a standard cover-letter section.
- Over-stylizing layout or using “artistic” formatting that reduces skimmability.

## Automation considerations

- No direct source found for “programmatic generation in an automation pipeline” as a dedicated topic, including CI-style generation architecture, templating systems, or automated testing of letters.
- Sources do discuss AI-assisted drafting and revision workflows, including precautions that apply to automated generation setups.
- Start from applicant-created material and use AI for feedback or editing, since Harvard and Stanford both warn against AI output as the primary author due to generic templates and weaker authenticity.
- Remove personally identifying information before sharing materials with AI tools, since multiple university sources frame privacy protection as a core practice.
- Provide the full job description to the tool and ask for top skills or requirements, then map examples from resume content to those skills, since Harvard outlines this approach for cover letters.
- Use targeted prompts for proofreading, tone review, and job-description coverage checks, since MIT describes structured prompt patterns for cover-letter editing.
- Review AI output for accuracy and avoid unsupported claims, since MIT describes risks such as overselling and hallucinated facts in AI-generated cover letters.
- Edit output to reduce recognizable AI templating, since Stanford warns recruiters recognize AI-generated templates and may reject materials perceived as chatbot-written, and UC Davis warns recruiters see repeated AI outputs across applicants.
- Check employer rules on AI use for application materials when a posting or employer guidance covers AI, since Harvard states some organizations set rules about AI use, and Microsoft states expectations around responsible AI use and honest representation during the recruiting process.
